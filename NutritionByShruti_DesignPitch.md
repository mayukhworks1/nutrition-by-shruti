## 1. Executive Summary

Shruti Maheshwari Baid is a credentialed Functional Medicine Practitioner — UC Berkeley educated, Head of Wellness at Poly Medicure Limited, with a track record of measurable, life-changing results: HbA1c dropped from 7 to 5.9 in three months, 60% client medication elimination, 80% improved digestion within two weeks. This is *extraordinary* clinical authority.

The problem is the website does not communicate any of it with the visual confidence it deserves.

The current site looks like a well-intentioned Wix template that got partially filled in. It is fragmented, visually inconsistent, and fails to convert the trust that Shruti's credentials and results naturally command. A visitor arriving from Google, Instagram, or a referral has no immediate reason to feel — viscerally — that they are in the hands of someone transformative.

This pitch proposes a full redesign direction: a premium, editorial wellness brand identity that positions Shruti not as a "dietician" but as a *movement* — functional medicine as a philosophy of life, delivered by someone exceptional. Every section of the redesigned site will serve one goal: **turn doubt into belief, and belief into a booked call.**

## 2. Current State Audit — What Is Broken and Why

### 2.1 Hero Section
**What it does:** Full-bleed grayscale photo (woman meditating) with large white serif headline "Start Your Journey to Improving Physical Health with Shruti Maheshwari Baid" and a pill-shaped "Learn More" CTA.

**The problems:**

**Stock photo with no emotional specificity.** The meditating woman in pink is the most generic wellness image possible — it could belong to a yoga studio, a supplement brand, or a hospital newsletter. It signals nothing about Shruti, her methodology, or her client outcomes. The image has zero storytelling power.

**Headline is transactional, not transformative.** "Start Your Journey to Improving Physical Health" is functionally identical to what every nutritionist in India writes. It describes a category, not a unique value. Contrast this with Organimo's "FROM BODY TO MIND — Limitless begins here." That copy draws you into a world. Shruti's copy sends you to a waiting room.

**The CTA "Learn More" is the weakest possible action.** It signals the site has something to say if you're patient enough to read it. The first CTA should be one of: *Book a Free Call*, *See My Results*, or *Find Out What's Keeping You Sick* — something that creates stakes and urgency.

**The hero image is blurred/loading slowly** — in both visits the right side of the hero image was blurry and unrendered. This is a performance and perception issue. Blurry images in a hero register subconsciously as unprofessionalism.

**The logo is pure typography, two-line stacked, far left, small.** "NUTRITION BY SHRUTI" in a plain serif has no visual identity. It does not communicate "premium functional medicine" — it reads as a student project.

---

### 2.2 Intrusive Email Popup on Load
**What it does:** The moment you arrive at the site, a large lightbox popup covers the hero with a "Your Inbox Deserves Better!" newsletter capture.

**The problem:** This is one of the worst UX decisions on the site. The visitor has seen nothing about Shruti, read no content, felt no trust, and is immediately asked to give their email. Conversion rate on these popups is typically 1-3%, while exit rate caused by them is often 15-25% on mobile. This popup should be:
- Triggered by **exit intent** only (when cursor moves to leave the page), OR
- Triggered after **60 seconds of dwell time**, OR
- A persistent bottom bar/slide-in — not a fullscreen takeover on first load.

The current implementation is the digital equivalent of someone blocking your door to hand you a flyer before you've even stepped inside the shop.

---

### 2.3 Stats Bar ("200+ Clients", "60% Elimination of Medications", etc.)
**What it does:** Four statistics in a horizontal row on a white background.

**The problem:** The numbers are genuinely powerful — but the design gives them no weight. They sit in a plain white section with no context, no visual hierarchy, and no supporting proof. A stat like "1.7 Point Drop in Average HbA1c" means nothing to a layperson unless it is contextualized ("That's the difference between managing diabetes and reversing it").

The numbers need: context copy beneath each stat, a dark or contrasting background to make them pop, and visual treatment (large typographic weight, accent color) that signals "these are results, not aspirations."

---

### 2.4 "We Help You Fix What Your Doctor Couldn't" Headline
**What it does:** A full-width centered headline in dark text on white.

**The honest assessment:** This is the **best line on the current website.** It is direct, provocative, and speaks directly to the frustrated patient who has been through conventional medicine without answers. But it is completely wasted — displayed as a lonely line with enormous white space above and below, no supporting copy, no imagery, no emotional anchoring. It reads like a placeholder for a section that was never finished.

This line should be the *centerpiece* of the hero section or the opening of a high-impact "Why Functional Medicine" section with supporting stats, a short video of Shruti speaking, and a direct CTA.

---

### 2.5 About / Bio Section
**What it does:** Left column: long bio text about Shruti's credentials. Right column: a single photo of Shruti at a desk.

**The problems:**

**The photo is too small relative to the person's authority.** Shruti is the product. Her face, her warmth, her credibility — these are the brand's primary trust asset. The photo should be large, confidently placed, high-quality, and ideally shot specifically for the brand (not a desk candid). Compare how a brand like Precision Nutrition presents its founder vs. how Shruti's photo sits here: small, under-lit, and tucked to the right.

**The bio is written like a LinkedIn profile**, not like copy that builds a human connection. "She has extensive experience working with Registered Nutritionists in the United States" is a résumé bullet. What it should say is something like: "Shruti watched her own family struggle with diabetes for years before she decided medicine had to do better. She went to Berkeley, she worked inside the US healthcare system, and she came back to India with one mission: to prove that food is the most powerful medicine we have."

**The title "Chief Medical Officer" is confusing** for a solo practitioner. Is this a clinic? A company? The visitor doesn't know. The title needs reframing — perhaps "Founder & Functional Medicine Practitioner."

---

### 2.6 "Exclusive Wellness" Slider
**What it does:** A half-image, half-text slider with arrows to navigate between service categories.

**The problem:** The slider (carousel) is one of the most consistently conversion-negative UI patterns in modern web design. Research from the Nielsen Norman Group consistently shows that most users never click carousel arrows — they see only the first slide. What's worse, the image used (food flatlay) has no emotional connection to the headline "Do mysterious symptoms puzzle you? Feeling constantly drained?"

The copy is actually good — it's emotionally resonant. But it's hidden in a slider that 80% of visitors will never see fully. These services need to be laid out as persistent visible cards.

---

### 2.7 Process Steps (4 Steps)
**What it does:** Four cards — STEP 1 through STEP 4 — with icons, titles, and short descriptions.

**The design assessment:** This section is structurally correct — a process/journey breakdown is exactly what a conversion-focused wellness site needs. But the execution is flat Wix template cards with thin strokes and generic icons. The steps are labeled with passive titles: "GATHERING INFORMATION," "EXPLORING YOUR HEALTH," "CREATING A LIFESTYLE," "SUCCESS SETUP."

These should be written from the client's perspective: "We Listen First," "We Map Your Root Causes," "We Build Your Personal Plan," "You Start Feeling Different." The language should make the client feel like an active protagonist, not a subject being processed.

---

### 2.8 "Foundations of Health" Section
**What it does:** Large images with overlay text for Nutrition, Movement, Sleep, Purpose — each linking to a deeper page.

**The problem:** Four identical-format stock photo tiles. The photography is generic and inconsistent in tone (some are bright and colorful, some are moody). This section has the potential to be the most visually impactful on the page — a grid of editorial lifestyle photography that says "this is what your life looks like when you work with Shruti." Instead it reads as a stock photo dump.

---

### 2.9 Services Cards ("Nutrition Insight" + "Your Journey, Personalized")
**What it does:** Two cards on a pale beige background with images, service names, descriptions, and "Explore Now" CTAs.

**The problem:** Both cards feel like Wix placeholder content. The image for "Your Journey, Personalized" (a bowl of nuts with a stethoscope) is a cliché medical-stock composite that undermines the premium positioning. There is no pricing anchor, no indication of transformation outcomes, and no social proof attached to the service. The visitor has no sense of what they're buying into.

---

### 2.10 Testimonials ("Journey to Joy")
**What it does:** Three testimonial cards with client photos and quotes.

**What it does right:** The testimonials are specific and results-oriented — Anil Agarwal's quote about his HbA1c and cholesterol is clinical gold. That specificity is what makes testimonials convert.

**What it does wrong:** The photos are blurry (privacy pixelation?) and the section title "Journey to Joy" reads soft and vague. The visual design — three equal-width photo cards — doesn't do justice to the power of these stories. These testimonials need: full names, designations or ages (to build relatability), a before/after structure where possible, and ideally a short video testimonial from even one client.

---

### 2.11 Color Palette & Typography
**Current palette:** Off-white / pale beige backgrounds, mauve-rose accent (for Shruti's name), dark charcoal for body text, bright green (#3a8a5a-ish) for "BOOK NOW" CTA button.

**The problem:** The green CTA button is visually jarring against the warm beige-and-rose palette. It was clearly added as an afterthought — "green = health" — without any consideration for brand cohesion. It creates a visual clash every time the eye lands on the header.

**Typography:** The site mixes a large serif (Georgia-adjacent) for headings with a sans-serif body — this is not inherently wrong, but the sizing hierarchy is inconsistent across sections. Some headings are 60px+, others are 28px, with no clear typographic system. The all-caps small labels ("STEP 1", "INDIVIDUAL DESIGN | ONLINE & IN-PERSON") are orphaned and not part of a consistent type scale.

---

### 2.12 Overall UX Issues Summary

**Navigation:** The top nav is functional but missing a "Free Consultation" or "Book Now" as a persistent sticky CTA on scroll. The moment the header scrolls away, there is no persistent entry point for a visitor who has just decided to act.

**Mobile performance:** Wix sites tend to perform poorly on mobile — the hero image loading blurrily, the popup, and the carousel-heavy layout are all compounded on smaller screens.

**No social proof in the fold:** The first thing a skeptical visitor needs is proof. The current site puts the hero first, then stats, then a long bio. Social proof (testimonials, media mentions, client results) should appear much earlier — ideally within the first three scrolls.

**No urgency or scarcity signal:** Nothing on the site communicates that Shruti's capacity is limited. "Book a Free 10-minute chat" appears only at the bottom. There is no "Currently accepting X new clients per month" or "Next availability: [date]" — mechanisms that drive action from people who are interested but not yet committed.

**WhatsApp chat widget** is the correct channel for this audience but it needs better placement and a smarter trigger. It should not pop open by default ("How can we help you?") before the visitor has expressed any interest — it creates visual noise in every screenshot of the site.

## 3. Brand Positioning — What This Brand Should Feel Like

Shruti's brand sits at the intersection of three powerful ideas:

**Clinical Authority** — She has Berkeley credentials, corporate experience (Poly Medicure), and published results (HbA1c reversal data).

**Human Warmth** — Her testimonials describe someone who "makes sure to work around my normal routine" and creates plans that are "doable, interesting, and beneficial." This is not a cold clinical practice.

**Eastern Wellness Philosophy** — The Indian context, the emphasis on food as medicine, the connection to Ayurvedic-adjacent thinking around digestion and wholeness — this is a huge differentiator from Western nutrition brands.

The brand should feel like: **a trusted elder who happens to have a Harvard-equivalent degree.** Warm, but authoritative. Rooted in science, but expressed in human terms. Aspirational but accessible.

Visual mood: **Warm cream and deep earth tones. Editorial photography. Confident whitespace. A typeface that commands respect.** Think the visual language of Kinfolk magazine meets a functional medicine clinic.

## 4. Reference Site Analysis

### 4.1 Organimo.com — The Premium Immersive Approach
**URL:** organimo.com
**Category:** Wellness supplement brand (Sea Moss & Bladderwrack)

**What it does brilliantly:**

The site opens with a cinematic preloader — a geometric gold triangle on near-black — and the line "FROM BODY TO MIND — Limitless begins here." Before you see a single product, you feel you are entering a world. This is intentional brand architecture: the preloader communicates premium before content loads.

The entire page uses a **scroll-driven narrative** — each section of copy appears as you move down, making the visitor feel like they are being guided on a journey rather than browsing a product page. The headline "The real limitless pill" is provocative, memorable, and positions the product in the mind before the rational brain catches up.

**Color system:** Near-black (#0d0d0d) background, gold/amber (#c9a84c-ish) as the only accent color. This is a two-tone system executed with discipline — every element serves it.

**What to borrow for Shruti:**

The idea of **scroll-driven, narrative-first design** is directly applicable. Rather than stacking sections, the redesigned site should feel like Shruti is *telling you a story* as you scroll — your symptoms, her journey, the method, the results, and finally the invitation to join.

The **single dominant brand line** — "Limitless begins here" — shows what one well-chosen brand statement can do. Shruti needs an equivalent: something like *"The doctor couldn't fix it. We will."* or *"Food is medicine. Let us show you."*

**The preloader / loading experience** — while too heavy for Shruti's brand — demonstrates that the *first impression* is a design decision. Even something as simple as a fade-in reveal of a full-bleed Shruti portrait sets a different tone than a blank white screen.

### 4.2 Precision Nutrition (precisionnutrition.com) — Authority + Transformation
**Category:** Nutrition coaching program, evidence-based

**What it does brilliantly:**
Precision Nutrition leads with its results ("We've helped 100,000+ clients") and immediately establishes the *transformation journey* with before/after-style narrative. Their site is dense with social proof — real client photos, specific outcomes, certifiable credentials. The photography is real people (not stock), which is a crucial trust signal.

Their "How It Works" section is the clearest process explanation I have seen in this category — each step is written from the client's emotional state, not the company's operational perspective.

**What to borrow for Shruti:**
The structure of **leading with outcome, not process.** Instead of "We do initial assessment → 60 min session → weekly sessions → supplements" (Shruti's current framing), the copy should open with what the client *feels after* the process — and then explain how you get there. Lead with the transformation, not the mechanism.

Also: **real client photography.** Shruti has real clients who have had life-changing results. Even three or four photographed clients with their full story (age, condition, outcome) would outperform all the stock photography currently on the site.

---

### 4.3 Noom (noom.com) — Conversion Optimization & Psychology-First UX
**Category:** Health & weight management app

**What it does brilliantly:**
Noom's homepage is a masterclass in CRO (Conversion Rate Optimization). The primary CTA is *not* "learn more" — it is a **quiz.** "Take our 5-minute quiz to find your personalized plan." This is genius: it turns passive reading into active participation, it collects data, and it creates commitment through the sunk-cost psychology of having already invested time.

Their copy is entirely problem-first, framed around psychology: "Why do diets fail?" — positioning them as the people who understand *why* you haven't succeeded before, which is infinitely more persuasive than listing credentials.

**What to borrow for Shruti:**
A **quiz or health assessment as the primary lead-generation CTA.** Instead of "Book a free 10-min chat" (which feels like a sales call and has high friction), a "Find out what's making you sick" or "Take our 3-minute health symptom audit" pulls visitors into the funnel with curiosity, not obligation. The quiz results can then recommend the right service tier and auto-route to a booking page.

### 4.4 Dr. Mark Hyman (drhyman.com) — The Expert Personal Brand
**Category:** Functional Medicine thought leader, author, physician

**What it does brilliantly:**
Dr. Hyman's site is the best-in-class example of a *personal brand as authority empire.* The hero is a large, confident portrait of Hyman — not stock, not a concept — just the expert, looking at you. The tagline is a philosophy statement: "Young Forever. Feel better than ever." The navigation includes Podcast, Books, Community, Programs — indicating multiple touch points.

Crucially, his site **integrates content (podcast/articles) with services seamlessly.** The blog isn't separate — it feeds back into his service pages, which feeds into his email list, which feeds into his programs. The site functions as an **ecosystem**, not just a brochure.

**What to borrow for Shruti:**
The **founder portrait as hero.** Shruti's face, her warmth, her credibility — that is the brand. There should be a confident, professionally lit photograph of Shruti as the first thing a visitor sees — ideally in a clean clinical-but-warm environment (not against a white wall, not a candid desk shot).

Also: the **content-to-services funnel.** Shruti already has blog posts ("Wellness Wisdom"). These should be deeply cross-linked with her services — a post about gut health should link naturally to her 3-month program; a diabetes article should reference her reversal case studies.

### 4.5 Zoe (joinzoe.com) — Data-Driven, Clean & Modern
**Category:** Personalized nutrition science (UK, Tim Spector)

**What it does brilliantly:**
Zoe uses clean, modern design — large whitespace, a primary blue + off-white palette, confident sans-serif typography — to make *nutrition science* feel accessible and exciting. They lead with their scientific credibility ("The world's largest nutrition science study") but wrap it in approachable language and design.

Their **testimonials section** is exceptional: video testimonials, specific outcomes, real names and occupations. Their service cards include specific differentiators — not just what they do, but what makes them different from everything else.

**What to borrow for Shruti:**
The **clean scientific aesthetic** balanced with warmth. Shruti's brand currently swings too far toward "soft wellness" (pale pink, serif fonts, food flatlays) and not enough toward "rigorous science." The redesign should thread both — medical credibility expressed in a warm human package.

Also: **Video testimonials.** Even one client video saying "I reversed my diabetes in three months" is worth ten written quotes.

### 4.6 MindBodyGreen (mindbodygreen.com) — Editorial Wellness Authority
**Category:** Wellness media & product brand

**What it does brilliantly:**
MBG's design is clean, editorial, and content-first. Their use of **white space** is confident — sections breathe, type is well-set, photography is art-directed (not stock). The brand feels like a premium magazine that sells products, not a product site that also has a blog.

Their navigation is a content hub — podcast, classes, supplements, articles — all positioned as equally legitimate entry points. This means visitors can come in from any direction and find relevant, valuable content immediately.

**What to borrow for Shruti:**
The **content-as-brand** philosophy. Shruti's "Wellness Wisdom" blog should be elevated to a proper editorial section — "The Journal" or "The Protocol" — with featured articles displayed like a magazine spread, not a three-card thumbnail row. This positions Shruti as a *thought leader*, not just a service provider.

## 5. Design Direction

### 5.1 Proposed Color System

**Primary:** Deep warm cream / sand — `#F5EFE0` (page backgrounds, large sections)
**Secondary:** Rich espresso / dark soil — `#2C1F14` (primary text, dark sections)
**Accent 1:** Terracotta / deep saffron — `#C4622D` (highlight, accent lines, hover states)
**Accent 2:** Warm sage green — `#6B7C5C` (secondary CTA, nature references)
**White:** Pure clean white `#FFFFFF` (cards, contrast sections)

**Rationale:** This palette is rooted in earth and warmth — it references Indian sensibility (terracotta, saffron, sage) without being clichéd. It signals both clinical authority (the clean cream and espresso) and human warmth (the terracotta accent). Critically, there is no jarring color conflict. Every element belongs to the same world.

---

### 5.2 Typography System

**Display Heading:** *Freight Display Pro* or *Cormorant Garamond* — a refined editorial serif for all H1s and major statements. This typeface should be used large and with confidence. It signals "this person has something important to say."

**Body Copy:** *DM Sans* or *Inter* — a clean, legible, neutral sans-serif for body text, captions, and UI elements. This ensures readability and a modern, clean feel.

**Accent / Label:** *DM Sans Italic* or *Cormorant Garamond Italic* in small caps for labels, taglines, and decorative elements.

**Type Scale:** Establish a clear 6-step scale: Display (80-100px), H1 (56px), H2 (40px), H3 (28px), Body Large (18px), Body (16px), Small/Caption (13px). Every section follows this scale — no more ad-hoc sizing.

---

### 5.3 Photography Direction

The single biggest upgrade to this brand is getting off stock photography. The directive for a photo shoot should be:

**Hero / About:** Shruti in a clean, warm-toned studio setting — a combination of looking directly at camera (authority) and in conversation or looking at work (approachability). Light should be natural/golden. Wardrobe should be smart-casual — not a white coat (too clinical), not casual wear (too informal).

**Process / Lifestyle:** Real clients in real contexts — a woman cooking in a kitchen, a man at his desk looking energized, an older woman walking. Not stock. Real faces with permission and stories.

**Food:** Editorial food photography — structured, intentional, not flatlays. Think more *bon appétit* editorial than Instagram meal prep.

**Background:** Remove all grey/grayscale backgrounds from lifestyle imagery. The palette should feel warm and alive.

## 6. Section-by-Section Redesign Strategy

### 6.1 Announcement Bar
**Current:** "Tired of random supplements? One scan shows what your body's missing — SCAN NOW"
**Recommendation:** Keep this — it is specific, timely, and ties to the OligoScan service. But change the background from grey to terracotta/accent color so it creates visual energy from the first pixel.

---

### 6.2 Navigation
**Current:** Logo (left) | Links (center-right) | BOOK NOW (green pill button)
**Recommended:**

Make the logo an actual mark — ideally a wordmark with a subtle visual element (a leaf, a cell, a circle motif) that can be used independently. "NUTRITION BY SHRUTI" in a confident single-line Cormorant Garamond.

Navigation links: simplify to 4 core items — About | Services | Results | The Journal — and consider renaming "Functional Medicine" to "Our Approach" (less jargon, more inviting for first-time visitors).

CTA button: change color from green to terracotta (matches palette). Add a secondary CTA — "Free Health Chat" as a text link next to the primary button for visitors who are not yet ready to commit.

Make the header **sticky/transparent-to-solid on scroll** — starts as transparent over the hero, then solidifies to cream/white as you scroll. This is standard premium-brand UX and immediately elevates the visual experience.

---

### 6.3 Hero Section
**Current:** Stock yoga image, generic headline, pill CTA.
**Recommended:**

Full-bleed **split composition** or **full Shruti portrait** as hero. Two possible directions:

*Direction A (Personal Brand):* 60% of the screen is a large, warm-toned portrait of Shruti. 40% is headline + CTA. Headline: "We help you fix what your doctor couldn't." Sub-headline: "Functional Medicine Practitioner | UC Berkeley | 200+ lives transformed." Primary CTA: "Book Your Free Health Call." Secondary CTA: "See Our Results →"

*Direction B (Cinematic):* Full-screen cinematic video loop (5-10 seconds) of Shruti in session, or nature/food B-roll, with text overlaid. Headline centered, large, Cormorant Garamond: "What if food was the only prescription you needed?" CTA below.

**Remove "Learn More" entirely.** Replace with action-specific CTAs that have stakes.

### 6.4 Social Proof / Trust Bar (ABOVE THE FOLD, not below)

A narrow dark bar with the four stats — but redesigned:
- `200+` clients transformed | `60%` eliminated medications | `80%` better digestion in 2 weeks | `1.7pt` average HbA1c drop

Use the espresso dark background, large Cormorant numerals in terracotta, small DM Sans labels. Make the numbers massive — 80-100px — so they land like facts, not footnotes. Add brief qualifying copy: "For our diabetes reversal clients" etc.

### 6.5 "Why Shruti?" / Problem Statement Section
**New section (currently absent):**

This is the section the current site is missing most. Before talking about the solution (Shruti's services), you need to name the problem the visitor is experiencing. This section should read like a mirror:

> *"You've been told your bloodwork is 'normal' — but you know something is wrong. You've been given prescriptions that treat the symptom, not the source. You're tired of feeling tired. You want answers, not bandaids."*

Then: *"That's exactly why functional medicine exists. And why we're different."*

Short copy. Big type. Dark background. Terracotta accent line. This section creates the emotional resonance that makes a visitor stop scrolling and say "this is about me."

Add: A short quote from Shruti in pull-quote style — something personal and direct: *"I watched conventional medicine fail my patients for years. I decided to do something about it."*

Replace passive titles ("GATHERING INFORMATION") with active, empathetic language: "We start by listening to everything your doctors skipped."

## 7. UX & Conversion Optimization Recommendations

**7.1 Replace homepage newsletter popup with exit-intent trigger.** Keep the email capture but trigger it when the user's cursor moves to leave the page or after 90 seconds of dwell time. This alone will measurably reduce immediate bounce rate.

**7.2 Add a sticky bottom bar on mobile.** On mobile, the primary CTA should be permanently visible — a slim bar at the bottom of the screen with "Book Free Call → " in terracotta. This is the highest-converting mobile UI pattern for service businesses.

**7.3 Add a "Quiz" or "Symptom Audit" as a primary lead-gen tool.** A 5-question interactive assessment ("How is your gut health?", "How often do you feel fatigued?", etc.) that ends with: "Based on your answers, you may benefit from [Service Name]. Here's how we can help." This dramatically reduces friction and increases email capture rates.

**7.4 Add urgency/scarcity signals.** A header bar element or CTA modifier: "Shruti accepts [X] new clients per month. [Y] spots remaining for July." This is accurate if capacity is genuinely limited — and for a solo practitioner, it almost certainly is.

**7.5 Speed optimization.** The current site loads hero images blurrily — a signal of unoptimized images on Wix. All above-the-fold images should be compressed, served in WebP format, and preloaded. Consider migrating away from Wix entirely to Webflow or Framer for better performance and design control.

**7.6 WhatsApp widget should be delayed.** Trigger the WhatsApp chat bubble after 30 seconds of dwell time, not immediately. Set the auto-message to something meaningful: "Hi! I'm Shruti's team. Let me know if you have any questions about our programs." Remove the default greeting "How can we help you?" as it sounds generic.

**7.7 Add a media/press section.** If Shruti has been featured in any media, blogs, or publications — even a single mention — add an "As Seen In" strip. If she hasn't, this is a near-term PR goal. Media logos are one of the fastest trust-builders available.

Both platforms allow pixel-perfect implementation of the design system described above. Wix will never fully deliver the premium aesthetic this brand deserves.
